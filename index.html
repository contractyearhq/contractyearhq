Most sports media has opinions. We have spreadsheets.

Contract Year was built on a simple frustration: sports media loves to call contracts good or bad, but almost nobody shows the math.

We come from finance. We know what it looks like when someone is overpaying for an asset, when a deal is genuinely undervalued, and when a front office has locked itself into a bad position for years. We apply that lens to NFL and NBA contracts — and we publish the work so you can check it yourself.

Every post ends with a verdict: Pay Him, Fair Deal, Overpaid, or Dead Money. We pick one. We defend it. And we welcome the argument in the comments — because that's the point.

Contract Year is @contractyearhq on Instagram. New ratings drop every Monday, Wednesday, and Friday during the season.

NBA · Boston Celtics · July 2023 Fair Deal
$304M.
The biggest contract in NBA history. Was it worth it?

In July 2023, Jaylen Brown signed the largest contract in NBA history — five years, $304 million, fully guaranteed, no player option. The Celtics had no real choice. Let him walk into free agency and you hand your competitor a top-10 player. Pay him what he earned — All-NBA honors, 26.6 points per game — and you keep your core intact.

So they paid. And then year one happened.

The Bull Case — why Boston pulled the trigger

Brown averaged 26.6 points and 6.9 rebounds in the season he earned this deal. He made second-team All-NBA. He was 26 years old. He was the co-centerpiece of a legitimate championship contender — and he proved that by winning Finals MVP in 2024, the first season of the supermax.

The Celtics had zero leverage. No comparable trade offer would have improved Boston's title window. Walking away meant handing a prime-years Jaylen Brown to a rival for nothing.

"At 26 with All-NBA credentials, you pay the number. That part isn't complicated."

The Bear Case — the red flags you can't ignore

Year one of the supermax: 22.2 points per game. Career low. His three-point percentage dropped to 32%. For a player earning $52M that season, the production-to-cost math wasn't pretty.

The real concern isn't year one or even year two. It's 2028-29, when Brown will earn $69.1 million at age 32. NBA history is not kind to guards earning max money in their early 30s. And there's no no-trade clause — the Celtics preserved the right to move him if things go sideways.

In March 2022, the Cleveland Browns signed Deshaun Watson to the only fully guaranteed quarterback contract in NFL history. Every dollar. Guaranteed. No performance clauses. No injury outs. $230 million, locked in, regardless of what happened next.

What happened next was twelve games, two major injuries, and a contract the Browns cannot escape under any circumstances.

The Bull Case — the best argument we could find

Before the suspension, Watson was a legitimate top-five quarterback. The Browns needed a franchise QB. They paid market rate for the player Watson was in 2022. Fully guaranteed contracts protect players — and that principle matters in a league where careers end without warning.

That's the entire bull case. It's not a long list.

"The Browns paid for 2022 Watson. They got 12 games."

The Bear Case — where to begin

Watson has appeared in 12 games since signing. That's it. Shoulder surgery, back issues — the body has failed him repeatedly. And because every dollar is fully guaranteed, the Browns cannot cut him, cannot trade him (no team will absorb this deal), and cannot create meaningful cap space while he sits.

The dead cap math is the most brutal part: if Cleveland releases Watson, $230M counts as immediate dead cap acceleration. They are trapped. There is no version of this where Cleveland wins.

When the Eagles signed Saquon Barkley in July 2024, the football analytics world collectively lost its mind. Paying a running back $37.75M per year — highest ever at the position — felt like market inefficiency in the wrong direction. The conventional wisdom said running backs are replaceable. The data said don't overpay the position.

Then Barkley ran for 2,005 yards, won the NFL rushing title, and collected a Super Bowl ring. The conventional wisdom needed to recalculate.

The Bull Case — 2,005 yards says it all

Barkley was the best running back in football in 2024. Not one of the best — the best. He won the rushing title. He helped carry Philadelphia to a championship. At 28 years old at signing, he was entering what should be his prime years, not his decline.

The Eagles also had no comparable replacement available. This wasn't an overpay for a fungible asset — it was market rate for an irreplaceable player at a critical moment.

"2,005 rushing yards erased every argument against this contract."

The Bear Case — the clock is ticking

Running backs age fast. Barkley turns 29 this year, and NFL history is littered with backs who fell off a cliff right around this age. The Eagles are locked into year 3 of this deal at $37.75M — and that's the one that could hurt.

In any other sport, paying a 37-year-old $59.6 million would be the beginning of the end conversation. In the NBA, when that 37-year-old is Stephen Curry — four rings, two MVPs, the greatest shooter who ever played — the math changes.

But does it change enough to justify 38% of Golden State's cap on a single player with no title window in sight?

The Bull Case — the greatest shooter alive

Curry is still averaging 26+ points per night and shooting above 40% from three at age 37. That's not a decline — that's sustained elite performance at an age where most players have retired or become role players. The Warriors don't just benefit from his basketball; they benefit from his revenue generation. He fills arenas even in losing seasons.

"Steph Curry isn't a team-building decision. He's a franchise decision."

The Bear Case — the age cliff hits without warning

No one predicts the age cliff until it arrives. Curry is 38% of Golden State's cap with no title window in sight. Klay Thompson is gone. Draymond Green is aging. The team being built around Curry cannot win a championship, and the cap is too constrained to significantly upgrade it while he's being paid like this.

Luka Doncic signed the largest contract in NBA history and then got traded to the Los Angeles Lakers — the contract came with him. $346 million. Five years. Now running the offense for the most storied franchise in basketball, at age 26, with the best years of his career still ahead of him.

The contract debate is easy. The roster debate is harder.

The Bull Case — generational talent at 26

Career averages: 26 points, 8 rebounds, 8 assists. At 26 years old. There are perhaps three players in NBA history with those career numbers — Oscar Robertson, Magic Johnson, and Luka Doncic. You pay that talent whatever it asks. No argument.

"The contract is fine. The front office has work to do."

The Bear Case — three seasons in Dallas produced zero conference finals

Luka's Dallas teams never reached the conference finals in his peak years. Questions about conditioning have followed him throughout his career. And at $69.2M per year, the Lakers have extremely limited flexibility to build a championship-caliber roster around him. You cannot win with one player at this price — you need two.

Bad Math is simple. You take an ugly number, show the calculation, and let the math speak. Tyreek Hill gives us one of the cleanest Bad Math cases in recent NFL history.

17 catches in his last 7 games. Tyreek Hill is making $30M per year. And he's told the Dolphins he wants more.

The Math — this is what we mean by bad math

$30M per year across a 17-game season works out to approximately $1.76M per game. Stretch that across 7 games and you get $12.4M in salary allocated to 303 receiving yards and 17 catches.

303 yards. $12.4M. That's $40,924 per receiving yard.

"Elite wide receivers average around $8,000 per receiving yard at comparable salary levels. Hill ran 5× that. This is Bad Math."

Our fair value estimate

Micah Parsons is the fastest player in NFL history to 50 career sacks. He has a 13.4% career pressure rate — elite by any measure. He has made multiple All-Pro teams. He is, by most accounts, the best edge rusher in professional football.

He is in the final year of his rookie contract and he wants to be the highest-paid non-quarterback in NFL history.

The Market — where the comps land

Aidan Hutchinson reset the market at $45M per year with Detroit. Myles Garrett sits at $40M. Maxx Crosby at $38.75M. TJ Watt, who has a reasonable argument as the best defensive player in football, was at $28M before his recent extension.

Parsons wants above Hutchinson. The Cowboys have been quiet. This is peak Dallas front office behavior — drag it out, act like they might not pay, then pay him anyway right before the season.

"57 sacks in 4 seasons. The résumé is undeniable. The Cowboys are just being Cowboys."
